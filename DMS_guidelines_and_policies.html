Data Management & Sharing (DMS): Definitions & Core Requirements

The National Institutes of Health (NIH) requires all applicants planning to generate scientific data to submit a Data Management & Sharing (DMS) Plan describing how their scientific data will be managed and shared.

1. Key Definitions

  • Data Management : This is the process of validating, organizing, protecting, maintaining, and processing scientific data to ensure its accessibility, reliability, and quality for users. It is crucial for maintaining scientific rigor and research integrity.
  • Scientific Data : The 2023 DMS Policy applies to all research funded or conducted in whole or in part by NIH, that results in the generation of scientific data. These are the results of research activities that are expected to be managed and shared. Examples include MRI images, genomic data, mobile data, and survey data. NIH does not expect all data to be shared, allowing researchers to decide based on ethical, legal, and technical factors.
  • FAIR Principles : NIH encourages data management and sharing practices to be consistent with these principles: Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable. Adhering to FAIR principles facilitates computer processing and analysis of datasets for secondary research.

2. Core Requirements for a DMS Plan

  • Submission : The DMS Plan must be attached as a PDF file in the "Other Plan(s)" field of the application forms (PHS 398 Research Plan or PHS 398 Career Development Award Supplemental Form).
  • Length and Format : DMS Plans are recommended to be two pages or less and must not include hypertext (e.g., hyperlinks and URLs).
  • Budget Justification : A brief summary of the DMS Plan and its associated costs must be included in the budget justification, labeled "Data Management and Sharing Justification". This justification should be no more than half a page and detail the data type, repository, general cost categories, amounts, and explanations.
    • Allowable Costs: include curating data, developing documentation, formatting, de-identifying, preparing metadata, local data management, and repository deposit fees. All allowable costs must be incurred during the performance period, even if they cover preservation beyond the award period.
    • Unallowable Costs: include institutional overhead, routine research conduct costs (like collecting data), and double-charged costs.
  • Assessment and Compliance : NIH program staff assess DMS Plans for completeness and reasonableness; funding is contingent on a complete and acceptable plan. Peer reviewers may comment on the reasonableness of the budget but these comments will not impact the overall score. Compliance with the approved plan is monitored annually.
